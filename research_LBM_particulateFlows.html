Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) - Particulate flows

Moving boundary

The LB method has received much attention in simulaing particulate flow, primarily because the fluid-solid interface can be easily implemented in the regular Cartesian grids, thereby improving the computational efficiency for moving particle simulations. Specifically, the no-slip boundary condition can be achieved by modifications on the density distribution function encountering the surface.

Drafting-kissing-tumbling

Drafting-kissing-tumbling (DKT) scenario was first observed by Fortes et al., 1987, J. Fluid Mech.: For two particle with equal sizes settling in a channel, the trailing particle will first accelerate in the wake of the leader. When they come near contact, their center lines will rotate, and the trailing particle will fall around the other and then take the lead.


An elliptical particle setttling in a narrow channel

An elliptical particle is settling in a narrow channel. Although the particle translates in the downward vertical direction, it rotates as if it was contacting and rolling up the near wall. This sedimentaion mode is known as "tumbling mode" Xia et al., 2009, J. Fluid Mech..
